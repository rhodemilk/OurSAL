Local Beaches

South Florida Beaches


The coastal sandy beach system along Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties is home to many species of plants and animals dependent upon the beaches, dunes, and nearshore waters for all or part of their lives. During the summer months, sea turtles come ashore to nest on Florida's beaches. There are more than 30 plants and animals considered rare within the state that inhabit the beach and adjacent habitats. The beach and dune system is the first line of defense against storms because it acts as a buffer between storm waves and coastal development or infrastructure. In addition, Florida’s beaches are a primary attraction for almost 19 million tourists each year.

Wildlife in Our Beaches


Florida Manatee

Florida manatees are large, slow-moving mammals with an elongated body and paddle-like flippers. They gather in Florida during the summer months. Even though manatees don’t have any natural predators, they are threatened by boat strikes, harmful algal blooms, disturbances by people, and more. The Florida manatee was on the federal endangered species list but has since gone up to the threatened list.

Bottlenose Dolphin

Bottlenose dolphins are naturally playful and curious animals that live for about 50 years. They can jump more than 50 feet high, and they are very smart. Bottlenose dolphins in the keys team up to chase schools of fish in very shallow water.

Nurse Shark

The nurse shark is also known as the sand tiger shark because of its spots. They can span up to 16 feet and have very strong jaws, with a strength of 43 pounds of pressure per square inch. Even though they have a fearsome look, they are harmless to humans.

Cannonball Jellyfish

Cannonball, or cabbage head, jellyfish are a main food source for the endangered leatherback sea turtle. It does not have any long tentacles that are usually associated with jellyfish; instead, it has a short oral arm.

Endangered Species

Threatened and Endangered Species List Florida Atlantic Coast

Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Loggerhead sea turtles are known for their large heads and strong jaws. They use their jaw muscles to feed on hard-shelled fish, like conch. Juvenile and adult loggerhead turtles live in US coastal waters. Florida’s sandy Atlantic and Gulf of Mexico beaches host one of the largest loggerhead nesting aggregations in the world.
The loggerhead sea turtle is protected as a threatened species by the Federal Endangered Species Act, as a federally-designated threatened species by Florida’s Endangered and Threatened Species Rule, and by Florida's Marine Turtle Protection Act (379.2431, Florida Statutes).

Smalltooth Sawfish

The smalltooth sawfish is closely related to the shark and ray. It has a long, toothy snout called the rostrum that takes up a quarter of its body. All species of sawfish are considered endangered and are experiencing a consistent population decline. In the United States, smalltooth sawfish are most often found off the southwest coast of Florida, from about Charlotte Harbor through the Everglades and Florida Keys region at the southern tip. The smalltooth sawfish is protected as an endangered species by the Federal Endangered Species Act and as a federally designated endangered species by Florida’s Endangered and Threatened Species Rule.

Threats to Our Beaches


Sargassum is a type of algae that floats in masses that never attach to the seafloor. It provides crucial habitat for many marine species, including endangered sea turtles, which, upon hatching on our beaches, make their way out to the sargassum to spend their early years feeding and growing amongst the algae. It is also an important element in shoreline stability. Over the past several years, South Florida and the Caribbean have experienced high levels of sargassum in coastal waters and on local beaches. Excessive amounts of sargassum in populated areas are causing concern worldwide. Prolonged exposure to hydrogen sulfide, which tends to occur in areas with decomposing sargassum, can cause upper airway irritation, nausea, headaches, vertigo, confusion, memory loss, and neurocognitive impacts.

Every year, Florida’s gulf coast faces harmful algae blooms named the Red Tide.
This bloom is caused by microscopic algae that produce toxins that kill fish and make shellfish dangerous to eat. The toxins make the surrounding air difficult to breathe. As the name suggests, the bloom of algae often turns the water red. Seabirds, sea turtles, manatees, and dolphins are all casualties of red tide. Between November 2022 and June 2023 alone, there were 123 manatee mortalities. Algal blooms can exacerbate pre-existing conditions like emphysema or asthma.

Pollution of coastal environments limits our ability to use beaches for economic, recreational, and aesthetic purposes. Pollution degrades and destroys the unique beach habitat needed by animals and plants. Any trash not recycled or properly thrown away can eventually reach our beaches when it is carried by the rain into storm drains, streams, and rivers and then flows into bays and estuaries all the way to the ocean and washes up on the beach.
Plastic is not biodegradable, and it does not disintegrate. Instead, plastics break down into microplastics. Microplastics are tiny pieces of plastic. Microplastics accumulate in the stomachs of marine animals. Even scarier is that microplastics make it into our food and water, which means humans are also consuming microplastics. It is estimated that 51 trillion microplastics have accumulated in the world's oceans. If plastics make it into our food and water, they enter the human food chain.

Most of the water for residents of northeast and central Florida comes from underground aquifers. The largest aquifer in the southeastern United States is the Floridan. Trash, sewage, litter, oil, chemicals, pesticides, and fertilizers can contaminate the water flowing to the aquifer. Increased construction, septic systems, and fertilizer applications can all introduce contaminants into water resources. Heavy rainfall, sandy soils, and porous limestone aquifers have the benefit of increasing the region's water supply, but some contaminants in the water cannot be filtered out through natural processes. The Florida aquifer is extremely vulnerable since it is close to the surface with no real barrier nestled between the land surface and aquifer, or is unconfined. Preventative management practices are crucial to maintaining water quality in these areas.

What Can YOU Do?

  • Use Plastic-Free Alternatives:

    Say no to single-use plastics like straws, plastic cutlery, coffee cups, water bottles, plastic bags, balloons, plastic-wrapped produce, and takeout food containers. Instead, opt for reusable alternatives like metal straws and reusable water bottles.



  • Correctly Discard Your Waste:

    Look for recycling bins in your community. Recycle clean and sorted items.

For any Heavy Recycling, Clean Out or Move out, Please place a Work order at 305-284-8282 (Coral Gables/RSMAS) or 305-243-6375 (Medical). For FOOD WASTE and Composting tips, visit miami.edu/gardens.

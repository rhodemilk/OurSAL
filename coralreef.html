Protecting Coral Reefs

What are Coral Reefs?

Corals are small marine animals that live in all oceans of the world. Together, they serve as an ecosystem characterized by their reef-building corals, or coral reefs. Coral reefs form some of the world's most productive ecosystems, providing complex and varied marine habitats that support a wide range of organisms. These organisms include various types of fish, sea turtles, clams, sea sponges, lobsters, and more. Healthy and resilient coral reefs can protect coastlines against hurricanes, shoreline erosion, and flooding.
However, coral reefs are being threatened by pollution, climate change, and other factors.

Florida's Coral Reefs

The only great barrier reef in North America is found in Florida. Florida's Coral Reef stretches approximately 360 linear miles from Dry Tortugas National Park west of the Florida Keys to the St. Lucie Inlet in Martin County. Dry Tortugas National Park is located at the southernmost point of Floridaâ€™s Coral Reef and offers some of the best views of this essential marine ecosystem. About two-thirds of Florida's Coral Reef lies within Biscayne National Park and the Florida Keys National Marine Sanctuary, a marine protected area that surrounds the Florida Keys island chain.

Coral Reef Threats

Stony coral tissue loss disease (SCTLD) is a highly lethal coral disease that was first reported off the coast of Florida in 2014 and has since spread rapidly throughout the Caribbean. Affected corals have a nearly 100% mortality rate. The disease affects over 20 coral species and is now present on reefs in 18 countries and territories. Until 2020, Dry Tortugas National Park was the only section to not show signs of the disease. But the disease was caught early enough that preventive treatment was possible. Only 3% of the corals at Dry Tortugas National Park are currently affected by the disease.

Pollution from trash and microplastics from improper disposal and stormwater runoff can greatly damage coral reefs. Trash such as plastic bags, bottles, and discarded fishing gear (also called marine debris) that makes its way into the sea can snag on corals and block the sunlight needed for photosynthesis, or entangle and kill reef organisms and break or damage corals. Degraded plastics and microplastics can be consumed by coral, fish, sea turtles, and other reef animals, blocking their digestive tracts and potentially introducing toxins.

More on Pollution

Increased greenhouse gases from human activities result in climate change and ocean acidification. Climate change is ocean change. The world's oceans absorb carbon dioxide (CO2), which helps slow global warming, but it is also changing the oceanâ€™s chemistry. Contributing factors that increase greenhouse gases in the atmosphere include burning fossil fuels for heat and energy, producing some industrial products, raising livestock, fertilizing crops, and deforestation. The effects on coral reefs are:

  • A warming ocean that contributes to coral bleaching and infectious disease (stony coral tissue loss disease).
  • Sea levels are rising, leading to increases in sedimentation for reefs located near land-based sources of sediment.
  • Sedimentation runoff can lead to the smothering of coral.
  • More frequent storm patterns that can cause the destruction of coral reefs.

How to Help

What can YOU do?

Recycle and dispose of trash properly (not in the ocean)
Marine debris can be harmful to coral reefs. Recycle your trash at home and on the go (especially plastic)
Reduce stormwater runoff in homes
Reduce rooftop runoff by directing your downspouts to vegetated areas and not to the storm drain on your street. If not, these storm drains carry out runoff water that picks up fertilizer, oil, pesticides, dirt, bacteria, and other pollutants. Runoff then makes its way through our streams, rivers, lakes, and the ocean. (which then pollutes our coral reefs).
Support and volunteer with organizations that are working to protect coral reefs.
Explore and discover local community organizations near you.

What Organizations & Local Governments Are Doing

Coral Reef Conservation Program

Coordinates research and monitoring, develops management strategies, and promotes partnerships to protect the coral reefs, hard-bottom communities, and associated reef resources along Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach, and Martin counties.

NOAA Coral Reef Conservation Program

Southeast Florida Coral Reef Initiative

The Southeast Florida Coral Reef Initiative (SEFCRI) Team first gathered in May 2003 to develop local action strategies targeting coral reefs and associated reef resources from Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach and Martin counties to improve the coordination of technical and financial support for the conservation and management of coral reefs.

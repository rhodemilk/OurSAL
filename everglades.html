The Everglades

What Are the Everglades?

Everglades National Park in southern Florida works to safeguard the Everglades, or the â€œRiver of Grass." The Everglades, the first national park established to conserve a biological system (1947), has also been named a UNESCO World Heritage Site, an International Biosphere Reserve, and a Wetland of International Importance.
The Everglades house unique plants and animals such as frogs, toads, alligators, hundreds of bird species, 300 fish species, Florida panthers, crocodiles, and snakes. The Everglades are subtropical wetlands with a freshwater system that begins near Orlando in the Kissimmee River. The water flows from the Kissimmee River to the small Lake Okeechobee, which is around 12 feet deep and encompasses 730 square miles. Historically, during the rainy season, water traveled from the lake to a slow-moving and shallow 50-mile-wide river that flowed across the Everglades sawgrass and into the Gulf of Mexico's mangroves.

Wildlife in the Everglades

The Everglades National Park has over 39 federally endangered or threatened native species. Here are a few featured animal cards for the Florida Everglades.

All Animals Living in the Everglades
List of Threatened and Endangered Animals

American Crocodile Endangered

The American crocodile is a reptile similar to the alligator yet very distinct. The American crocodile has a lizard-like shape, a long, muscular tail, and four short legs with five toes on the front and four on the back. Fully grown crocodiles have grayish green backs and tails with white to yellowish bellies. Their small nose is triangular, and the fourth tooth on both sides of the lower jaw can be seen when the mouth is closed. The American crocodile is currently endangered due to illegal hunting and habitat loss.

American Alligator Threatened

The Everglades is the only site in the world where crocodiles and alligators coexist in the wild. Alligators can be identified from American crocodiles by their head shape and color. When alligators close their jaws, their snouts are large and rounded, with no bottom teeth visible. Adult alligators are dark gray in appearance with a light underbelly. The primary threat to the American alligator is the destruction of their wetland habitat.

Florida Panther Endangered

The Florida panther is a large, long-tailed cat who primarily lives in the drier parts of the Everglades. Even at the best estimates, the Florida panther is one of the rarest and most endangered mammals in the U.S. and is protected under both the Endangered Species Act and Florida law. Habitat reduction and human interactions are their primary threats.

White Ibis

White Ibises live in a variety of wetlands, but mainly the Everglades. The primary threat to the white ibis is the loss of wetland habitat as a result of human development in coastal areas and freshwater feeding grounds.

Mangrove

The mangroves in Everglades National Park are a vital habitat and nursery for a wide array of important marine species, such as wading birds and fish like the red drum and spotted seatrout, as well as for endangered and threatened species. Mangroves help maintain water quality by filtering pollutants and trapping sediment.

Invasive Species

Invasive species overpopulate and destroy the natural order of the animals and habitat in the Everglades. These species usually harm large amounts of other species, causing some to be endangered or threatened. Here are some invasive species in the Everglades:

Burmese Python

The Burmese python is a nonvenomous snake native to the jungles and marshes of Southeast Asia. Pythons can also be found in Africa, Australia, and now Florida. Pythons were imported from Southeast Asia by the tens of thousands for the exotic pet trade, and escaped pets established a breeding population in Florida by the mid-1990s. Pythons are notorious for eating anything, even an American alligator. There is currently a Python Elimination Program being utilized to help reduce the invasive species population.


Python Elimination Program

Cuban Tree Frog

Cuban tree frogs devour native tree frogs as well as insects that would otherwise be eaten by native species. They are not only prevalent in the Everglades but also throughout northern Florida. When handled, Cuban treefrogs produce a toxic skin fluid that is particularly irritating to humans' mucous membranes (eyes and nose). Their high-pitched breeding calls are often deemed a nuisance.

Water Safety

COol Fact:

One out of every three Floridians relies on the Everglades for their drinking water supply. Water from the Everglades National Park drains into the Biscayne Aquifer, which supplies drinking water for Dade, Broward and Palm Beach County communities.

The Everglades is not a swamp but a very slow and shallow river. The Everglades were a low nutrient habitat that depended more on rainfall and flow from Lake Okeechobee. Even small amounts of nutrients, such as phosphorus, can upset the ecological balance needed by the native plants and animals. The present Everglades has been subdivided by the construction of canals, levees, roads and other facilities and has resulted in lost connections between the central Everglades and adjacent transitional wetlands. This separation and isolation can impair the Everglades' wildlife communities and sustainability of the ecosystem. Excess phosphorus is regularly carried across the landscape in stormwater runoff from local urban and agricultural drain systems, harming natural areas by promoting algae growth and an overabundance of non-native plants, crowding out natural vegetation and disrupting food sources and habitats used by native wildlife.
